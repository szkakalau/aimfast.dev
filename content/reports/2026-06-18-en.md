## 📝 Editor's Note

The hottest product on Product Hunt today is Goldfish (606 upvotes), an AI assistant that helps you reply just by pressing the Option key. Everyone's talking about "how to make AI sound more like you." But the truly buildable signals are hiding in two smaller products: **DevCleaner** and **mattpocock/skills**.

The second tells you that writing "skills" for AI agents (software that can call tools on your behalf) is turning into a real business — mattpocock's `.claude` skills pack has 133K stars, and a "taste-skill" project can help you avoid generating boring code.

Who will pay first? **Indie developers using Cursor/Claude Code.** Why this week? Because Anthropic's Fable model just got roasted for being too expensive (26 discussions). Developers are paying for LLM subscriptions while watching their hard drives fill up with cache — the pain is compounding.

Is a `$19` "AI Dev Tool Disk Cleanup Report" worth it? If it helps a user reclaim 30GB of space, that's like saving a month of `$10` iCloud storage upgrade fees. The real hard work isn't writing a cleanup script — it's **identifying which caches are safe to delete**. That requires a manually maintained "known cache directories" list.

**Pricing**:

**Fastest Validation Path**:

**Keep the MVP Manual**: The first version is just a Google Form + Markdown report. Users fill out the form, you manually analyze and send the report. Don't jump straight into writing an auto-scan script.

## 🔍 Opportunity Discovery

### Solo-founder Product Launches

**DevCleaner: Free the gigabytes your dev tools and AI apps hoard**
- 🔍 **Signal**: Product Hunt 111 upvotes, 18 comments, score 36.
- **Plain English**: A Mac menu bar app that finds and cleans caches left by Cursor, Claude Code, Docker, and other dev tools. Not a "general Mac cleaner" — it's "specifically for dev tools."
- **Key Judgment**: This is a **feature**, not a **company**. But it's good enough as an MVP to validate product demand. If validated, it could expand into a "DevOps Disk Optimization Suite."
- **Reverse Perspective**: If users are just "liking to support" rather than "paying to buy," this signal is invalid. If more than 5 of the 18 comments say "this is built into the system," the product is dead.

**MakersClaw: Hire AI employees that live in your Slack, Teams, Telegram**
- 🔍 **Signal**: Product Hunt 403 upvotes, 57 comments, score 32.
- **Plain English**: Put AI agents as "employees" into your chat tools (Slack, Teams, Telegram). They can execute tasks and answer questions.
- **Key Judgment**: Another attempt at the "AI employee" concept. Previous similar products (like Adept, Inflection) failed because "AI employee" was too vaguely defined. If MakersClaw focuses on "auto-replying to common questions in Slack," it might succeed more easily than a "universal employee."
- **Reverse Perspective**: Many of the 57 comments are asking "What's the difference between this and Zapier?" If the answer is just "smarter," then Zapier holds the pricing power.

### What Developers Are Complaining About

**Anthropic's Fable model is too expensive**
- 🔍 **Signal**: HN 26 comments, 17 upvotes, score 26.
- **Plain English**: Developers are complaining that Anthropic's new Fable model is too expensive. It might be powerful, but the price is prohibitive.
- **Key Judgment**: This is a persistent signal — "models are expensive" is an eternal pain point for indie developers. This creates two opportunities: 1) Model routing (automatically choosing the cheapest model for the current task, like Edgee Turbo Models); 2) Model cache optimization (reducing repeated calls to save costs).
- **Reverse Perspective**: People complaining about "expensive" aren't necessarily willing to pay for a "cost-saving solution." Many developers would rather write their own optimization scripts than pay for someone else's tool.

**Side project Chrome plugin: from $0 to $1500/month in 4 months**
- 🔍 **Signal**: w2solo 28 points, in-depth sharing article.
- **Plain English**: A developer spent a weekend building a Chrome plugin (web highlighter and note-taker). His strategy: 1) Launch on Product Hunt and Hacker News; 2) Write a blog about "how I promoted it" (the article itself is promotion); 3) Do SEO.

His experience shows that for tool-type products, content marketing (especially "how to promote" meta-content) is the cheapest way to acquire users.
- **Reverse Perspective**: $1500/month might be a ceiling that's easy to defend but hard to break through. Chrome plugins depend on Web Store search rankings. If policies change or a strong competitor appears, revenue could drop to zero.

## 🏭 Competitive Intelligence

### Indie Developer Revenue & Pricing Discussions

**Chrome plugin: $1500/month in 4 months**
- 🔍 **Signal**: w2solo 28 points.
- **Plain English**: Analyzed above. This is an important pricing reference point. $1500/month ≈ 60 lifetime license users at `$25`, or 150 subscription users at `$10/month`.
- **Key Judgment**: For tool-type Chrome plugins, a `$25` lifetime license is easier to sell than a `$10/month` subscription. Users have a more relaxed mental accounting for "buying once."

**Third year of remote work: salary dropped from 30K to 15K per month**
- 🔍 **Signal**: w2solo 24 points.
- **Plain English**: A remote developer shared that while their monthly salary dropped from 30K to 15K, their hourly rate doubled because they worked half the hours.
- **Key Judgment**: This signals a mindset shift among indie/remote developers. They're no longer chasing "high monthly salary," but "high hourly rate." This means **tools that save them time are easier to sell than tools that help them make more money**. Pricing logic should shift from "helps you earn more" to "helps you save time."
- **Reverse Perspective**: This is survivorship bias. Not everyone can double their hourly rate from $50 to $100. Many remote workers are seeing their income decline.
